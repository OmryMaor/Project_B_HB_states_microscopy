 1 Abstrac t 
In this project, we investigated methods for improving the resolution in electron 
microscopy using quantum information under a radiation dose limit. Initially, we explored 
spin squeezing techniques that allow overcoming the classical limit (SQL), but we shifted 
our research direction to multipixel phase estimation, since practically, the phase 
estimation of the electron  in a microscope  occurs across multiple  pixels in parallel.  
This solution is based on paper  [1], which demonstrates that the simultaneous estimation 
of multiple phases provides an asymptotic advantage of 𝒪(𝑑) over the separate 
estimation of each phase. Subsequently, we ran simulations and optimized the quantum 
states under a noisy channel model involving particle loss in a beam splitter with a single 
environmental mode, as well as a beam splitter with an envir onmental mode for each 
particle  independently . 
The results show that multipixel phase estimation dramatically improves the resolution for 
a given radiation dose, and that the obtained optimal states correspond to the ideal states 
from paper  [1] with high overlap.  
In doing so, we essentially demonstrated that under a simple noise model, a significant 
quantum advantage can be achieved in particle phase estimation under a radiation dose 
constraint – an achievement of great importance in various scientific field s.

Figure 1: The challenge of obtaining a high -resolution image without damaging the material  2 Introduction and Motivation  
Efficient microscopy is a critical requirement in biology. Transmission Electron 
Microscopes (TEMs) can achieve atomic resolution; however, they are highly limited by 
beam -induced radiation damage. Consequently, imaging sensitive samples requires an 
extrem ely limited electron dose, which fundamentally degrades image precision.  
The overarching goal of the research team is to improve electron microscopy using 
quantum technology. Specifically, the global objective is to maximize image resolution 
under a finite dose by optimally utilizing the available phase information extracted fr om 
every single electron. Classical phase estimation is bounded by shot -noise limits; 
however, by exploiting quantum entanglement between electrons, one can surpass these 
classical bounds and approach fundamental quantum limits, dramatically improving the 
signal -to-noise ratio (SNR) for a given dose.  
Within this larger framework, image formation must be treated as a multiparameter 
problem, as a single specimen imparts varying local phase shifts across a two -
dimensional spatial grid of pixels. Our specific goal in this project was to find numerically 
optimized quantum states for multipixel phase estimation in a realistic, noisy environment. 
By simulating a multi -mode interferometer subjected to mixed noise channels , 
incorporating both independent particle loss and correlated electron loss , we aimed to 
identify the quantum states that yielded the lowest bound on the estimated phase’s 
variance . Identifying these optimal states under realistic noise lays the theoretical 
groundwork for future experimental efforts to implement quantum -enhanced resolution in 
practical electron microscopy.

To mathematically evaluate the potential precision of a quantum state, we utilize the 
Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) matrix, denoted as  𝐼𝜃. The precision of multiparameter 
estimation is governed by the quantum Cramér -Rao Bound (CRB), which dictates that 
the covariance matrix of the estimated phases is lower -bounded by the inverse of the QFI 
matrix: 𝐶𝑜𝑣 (𝜽)≥𝐼𝜃−1. By maximizing the trace of the QFI matrix, we minimize the overall 
phase variance across all pixels, effectively minimizing the average error in the 
reconstructed image.  
3.2 Spin Squeezing in Electron Microscopy  
A prominent quantum metrological approach to surpassing the SQL is spin squeezing, 
which redistributes quantum uncertainty via multi -particle entanglement to reduce 
fluctuations in the phase variable of interest [2]. We initially explored two primary methods 
for generating spin -squeezed electron states in a Mach -Zehnder Interferometer (MZI): 
Interaction -Based Squeezing , which exploits collective Coulomb repulsion, and 
Measurement -Based Squeezing , which functions as a quantum non -demolition (QND) 
measurement.

estimation framework to a realistic noise model.  
We modeled  environmental noise strictly as a particle loss channel implemented via 
beam -splitter interactions. We specifically chose to attribute this loss to mechanisms like

 6 Conclusions  
6.1 Multipixel estimation dramatically improves resolution 
for a given dose  
Throughout this project, our primary constraint was the severe radiation dose limit 
imposed by sensitive biological samples in transmission electron microscopy. To 
overcome this, our specific objective was to numerically find and evaluate optimal 
quantum i nput states that maximize extracted phase information under realistic, noisy 
microscope conditions.  
By evaluating the performance of these numerically optimized states across our 
discretised model, we successfully demonstrated that simultaneous multipixel estimation 
fundamentally outperforms classical strategies. Specifically, adapting our states to the 
loss channel provided a robust variance advantage over both the classical Standard 
Quantum Limit (SQL) and separate independent estimation strategies. Ultimately, we 
confirmed that by actively optimizing correlated quantum states for a lossy environment, 
we can extract significantly more phase information from the exact same number of 
electrons, dramatically improving the allowable image resolution before beam -induced 
damage occurs.

 7 Future Work  
While our numerical model successfully demonstrated a quantum advantage under mixed 
particle loss channels, we identified several avenues for expanding this framework toward 
physical implementation. Based on our simulation results and the subsequent analys is 
during our project reviews, we identified the following directions for future research:  
7.1 Investigating the Correlation Between 
Transmission and Phase  
It is known that there is a correlation between an object’s transmission properties and the 
phase accrued by it. Throughout our work, we noticed that perhaps investigating this 
correlation could help finding more fitting and steady optimal input states that are tailored 
to the specific specimen inspected.  
7.2 Exploring Alternative Robust States  
Our optimization algorithm actively searched for the absolute optimal states that 
maximized the Quantum Fisher Information within the loss channels. However, we 
proposed that future research should also explore other, more general initial states. 
These sub optimal states might be mathematically or physically simpler to prepare in an 
actual microscope, while still possessing  an advantage over single -parameter estimation 
methods.  
7.3 Applying Different Evaluation Metrics  
To evaluate how the loss channel degraded our optimized states, we primarily calculated 
their fidelity to the theoretical noiseless optimum. We concluded that future studies should 
compare the optimized states using alternative distance measures and perfor mance 
metrics beyond pure fidelity. Analyzing the states through different mathematical lenses 
could yield deeper insights into their structural robustness under severe noise.
